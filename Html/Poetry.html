Poetry

A Glimpse into Various Forms of Poetry

“I have never started a poem whose end I knew. Writing a poem is discovering.” - Robert Lee Frost
Poetry is a form of literature that involves the interplay of words and rhythm. Words are weaved together to form sounds, images, and ideas that would be too complex to describe directly. .

Rhyming poetry

These poems use words that have similar-sounding syllables. In particular, the vowel sounds of the syllables match while the consonant sounds do not. There are many rhyming schemes for poems but the ABAB, ABCB, AABBA and AABB schemes are the most common.
“No Man is an Island” by John Donne, “Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening” by Robert Frost, “Shall I Compare Thee to a Summer's Day?” by William Shakespeare and “I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud” by William Wordsworth are some famous rhyming poems.

Blank Verse

These are poems with a consistent rhythmic structure (metre) written in iambic pentameter (line of verse with five metrical feet). They are not rhyming poems. Christopher Marlowe was the first English author to receive critical notoriety for the use of blank verse. Blank verse was introduced into England by the Earl of Surrey in about 1540. It is the principal metre of Shakespeare’s plays and the metre of Milton's epic poems and many other major works of poetry. .

Free verse

Free verse poetry lacks a rhyming scheme, metrical pattern and musical form. This gives freedom to the poets unlike blank verse, where they are bound to strict metrical forms.
Free verse was originally a literal translation of “vers libre”, a movement in France in the 1880s that liberated French poetry from its traditional prosodic rules. American poet Walt Whitman is often called the father of free verse. .

Epics

They are lengthy, narrative poems that typically deal with characters’ extraordinary feats and adventures from the distant past. Some popular examples of epics include.
Homer’s The Iliad and The Odyssey, both of which detail the events of the Trojan War and King Odysseus’s journey home from Troy.
The Mahabharata is an ancient Indian epic composed in Sanskrit. Scholars believe its subject matter might be dating back to the eighth or ninth century BC. At over 200,000 lines, it is considered the longest poem ever written. It also contains prose mixed in with poetry.
The Divine Comedy was an epic poem by Dante Alighieri that was completed in 1320. The poem imagines Dante travelling through Hell, Purgatory, and finally Heaven, in sections titled Inferno, Purgatorio, and Paradiso.

Narrative poetry

Narrative poems contain all the elements that make up a story, including characters, plot, conflict, and resolution. This form of poetry grew out from oral traditions.

It was favoured by medieval poets, most notably exemplified by Geoffrey Chaucer's “The Canterbury Tales”, which is a collection of 24 narrative poems. Other examples are “The Raven” by Edgar Allen Poe,” The Ring and the Book” by Robert Browning, and “The Faerie Queene” by Edmund Spenser.

Haiku

Haiku is a form of Japanese poetry made of short, unrhymed lines that evoke natural imagery.
Traditional Japanese haiku consists of three phrases that contain a kireji, or "cutting word" and a kigo, or seasonal reference. Out of the three lines, with 17 syllables in total, the first line is 5 syllables, the second line is 7 syllables and the third line is 5 syllables. Haiku was originally used to describe nature and seasons.
The haiku below is called “The Old Pond” by Matsuo Bashō (translated)-
“Old pond… a frog jumps in water’s sound”

Pastoral poetry

A pastoral poem explores the fantasy of withdrawing from modern life to live in an idyllic rural setting. Such poems usually have a beautiful, natural setting and have religious and political allegory. It runs on the belief that country life is better than urban life.

Pastoral poetry originated in the Greek Hellenistic period when the poet Theocritus wrote about rural life in the countryside. An early and influential pastoral work in the English language was Edmund Spenser’s “The Shepheardes Calender” (1579).

Sonnets

These are 14 line poems that traditionally consist of an octave (8 line stanza) and a sestet (6 line stanza). The two most common sonnet variations are the Italian sonnet (Petrarchan sonnet), and the English sonnet (Shakespearean sonnet). The main difference between the Italian and English sonnet is the rhyme schemes they use. Shakespeare has written 154 sonnets of which “Sonnet 18” and “Sonnet 80” are the most popular. Francesco Petrarch has written over 300 sonnets of which "Sonnet 131" is the most well-known.

Elegies

An elegy is a poem that reflects upon death or loss. It contains themes of mourning, loss, and reflection and it can also explore themes of redemption and consolation. It is a quatrain poem with an ABAB rhyme scheme and is written in iambic pentameter.

Some examples of elegiac poems are - John Donne’s “The Flea”, Alfred, Lord Tennyson’s “In Memoriam” (1850), and Percy Bysshe Shelley, “Adonaïs: An Elegy on the Death of John Keats” (1821).

  • Learn More

Ode

An ode is a short lyrical poem that praises an individual, an idea, or an event. An ode is traditionally divided into three sections-
The strophe-two or more lines repeated as a unit.
The antistrophe- has the same structure as a strophe and offers a thematic counterbalance.
The epode summarizes or concludes the ode.

An ode may be Pindaric, Horatian, or Irregular (or Cowleyan). ” To Autumn” by John Keats, ”Ode on Solitude” by Alexander Pope, and “Ode to the West Wind” by Percy Bysshe Shelley are some well-known odes.

Limerick

A limerick is a five-line poem that consists of a single stanza, an AABBA rhyme scheme. Most limericks are comedic or downright crude, and nearly all are trivial. Among the most famous of these is the opening poem from “A Book of Nonsense”-
There was an Old Man with a beard,
Who said,” It is just as I had feared!
Two Owls and a Hen, Four Larks and a Wren
Have all built their nests in my beard!"

Poems leave an emotional impact on the reader. Poetry creates awareness on social issues such as religious intolerance, poor sanitation, pollution, etc. For example, the poem “The Cold Within” by James Patrick Kinney throws light on racial and religious intolerance and the lack of compassion in people, and the “Anthem for Doomed Youth” by Wilfred describes the horrors of war. Writing and reading poems also serves as a means to express ourselves and decrease the feeling of isolation during this pandemic.
